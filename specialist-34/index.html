chapter 1 | functions and graphs

addition of ordinates

graphs of rational of low degree can be sketched by a combination of addition of ordinates and calculus to find the coordinates of any stationary points

reciprocal functions

graphs of simple quotient functions can be sketched by a combination of checking for zeros in the numerator and denominator in order to find intercepts and asymptotes and the use of calculus to find the coordinates of any stationary points. in particular, the graph of

$$y=\frac{1}{ax^2+bx+c}$$

can be sketched by first sketching the parabola given by \(y=ax^2+bx+c\) and then using the reciprocals of the ordinates. note that if the parabola has x-axis intercepts, the reciprocal curve will correspondingly have vertical asymptotes through these points

absolute function

the absolute value function of \(x\) is written as \(\lvert x \rvert\) and is defined as

$$\lvert x\rvert=\begin{cases}x&,x\geq 0\\-x &,x\lt0\end{cases}$$

the graph of the absolute value function, given by \(y=\lvert x\rvert\), is therefore equivalent to the graphs of \(y=x\) for \(x\geq 0\) and \(y=-x\) for \(x\lt 0\) sketched on the same set of axes

reciprocal circular functions

$$\csc x=\frac{1}{\sin x}$$

$$\sec x=\frac{1}{\cos x}$$

$$\cot x=\frac{1}{\tan x}$$

and they satisfy the identities:

$$\csc^2 x = 1 + \cot^2 x$$

$$\sec^2 x = 1 + \tan^2 x$$

compound angle formulas

$$\sin(x\pm y)=\sin x\cos y\pm\cos x\sin y$$

$$\cos(x\pm y)=\cos x\cos y\mp\sin x\sin y$$

$$\tan(x\pm y)=\frac{\tan x\pm\tan y}{1\mp\tan x\tan y}$$

double angle formulas

$$\sin(2x)=2\sin x\cos y$$

$$\cos(2x)=\cos^2 x-\sin^2 x$$

$$\tan(2x)=\frac{2\tan x}{1-\tan^2 x}$$

inverse circular functions

the inverse circular functions \(\sin^{-1}x\), \(\cos^{-1}x\) and \(\tan^{-1}x\) also known by their alternative names (\(\arcsin\), \(\arccos\) and \(\arctan\)) are defined by:

$$\sin^{-1}:[-1,1]\to R,\sin^{-1}x=y,-\frac{\pi}{2}\leq y\leq\frac{\pi}{2}$$

$$\cos^{-1}:[-1,1]\to R,\cos^{-1}x=y, 0\leq y\leq\pi$$

$$\tan^{-1}:R\to R,\tan^{-1}x=y,-\frac{\pi}{2}\leq y\leq\frac{\pi}{2}$$

chapter 2 | algebra

partial fractions

rational functions of a real variable:

$$\frac{ax+b}{(x-c)(x-d)}=\frac{A}{x-c}+\frac{B}{x-d}$$

$$\frac{ax+b}{(x-c)^2}=\frac{A}{x-c}+\frac{B}{(x-c)^2}$$

for the rational function \(\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\) where \(q(x)\) is a quadratic with real, linear factors and \(p(x)\) is a polynomial of degree 2 or more, use long division to write \(\frac{p(x)}{q(x)} = d(x)+\frac{r(x)}{q(x)}\) where \(r(x)\) is linear; then apply partial fractions to \(\frac{r(x)}{q(x)}\)

complex numbers

\(C\) is the set of complex numbers \(z\) given by \(z=x+yi\), where \(x,y\in R\) and \(i^2 = -1\)

component form

the real part of \(z=x+yi\) is denoted by \(Re(z)\) and is given by \(Re(z)=x\)

the imaginary part of \(z=x+yi\) is denoted by \(Im(z)\) and is given by \(Im(z)=y\)

complex equal

two complex numbers are equal if and only if their real parts are equal and their imaginary parts are equal

pure imaginary

a complex number of the form \(ki\), where \(k\) is real and non-zero, is known as a pure imaginary number

complex addition

if \(z=a+bi\) and \(w=c+di\), then:

$$z\pm w=(a\pm c)+(b\pm d)i$$

$$zw=(ac-bd)+(ad+bc)$$

complex conjugate

the complex conjugate of \(z = x + yi\), written as \(\bar{z}\), is given by \(\bar{z} = x - yi\), and then:

$$z+\bar{z}=2x=2Re(z)

a real number

$$z-\bar{z}=2yi=2Im(z)i$$

an imaginary number

$$z\bar{z}=(x+yi)(x-yi)=x^2+y^2$$

a real number

complex simplify

to divide non-zero complex numbers \(z_1\) and \(z_2\), multiply the numerator and the denominator by the complex conjugate:

$$\frac{z_1}{z_2}=\frac{z_1\bar{z_2}}{z_2\bar{z_2}}=\frac{z_1\bar{z_2}}{\lvert z_2\rvert^2}$$

argand diagram

the two-dimensional plane with perpendicular axes \(Re(z)\) and \(Im(z)\), called an Argand diagram (or Argand plane), is used to give a geometrical representation of the numbers in \(C\)

complex modulus

the modulus of a complex number \(z\), \(\lvert z\rvert\), is the distance in an Argand diagram from the origin to the point representing \(z\), i.e.

$$\lvert z\rvert=\lvert x+yi\rvert=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$$

$$\lvert z\rvert^2=x^2+y^2=z\bar{z}$$

principle argument

if \(z\) is a non-zero complex number, so that a ray joining the origin \(O\) to the point in an Argand diagram representing \(z\) makes an angle of \(\theta\) with the positive direction of the \(Re(z)\) axis, then the angle \(\theta\) is known as the argument of \(z\) and is written as \(arg(z)\); if in addition \(-\pi\lt\theta\leq\pi\), then \(\theta\) is known as the principle argument of \(z\) and is written as \(Arg(z)\)

polar form

the polar form of the complex number \(z\) is given by

$$z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)\text{ or }z=r\text{cis}\theta$$

$$r=\lvert z\rvert\text{, }r\cos\theta=Re(z)\text{ and }r\sin\theta=Im(z)$$

complex product/quotient

if \(z_1=r_1\text{cis}\theta_1\) and \(z_2=r_2\text{cis}\theta_2\) are non-zero complex numbers, then:

$$z_1z_2=r_1r_2\text{cis}(\theta_1+\theta_2)$$

$$\frac{z_1}{z_2}=\frac{r_1}{r_2}\text{cis}(\theta_1-\theta_2)$$

De Moivre's theorem

$$z^n=(r\text{cis}\theta)^n=r^n\text{cis}(n\theta)$$

conjugate root theorem

the fundamental theorem of algebra states that a polynomial function of a single variable \(z\) with complex coefficients has at least one linear factor in \(C\). the conjugate root theorem states that if a polynomial equation has only real coefficients, then any complex roots occur in conjugate pairs

complex relations

sets of points in the complex plane such as lines, rays, circles, ellipses and other curves can be described by conditions set on the complex number \(z\), including:

$$\lvert z-a\rvert=c$$

where \(a\) is complex and \(c\) is real, describes a circle centre \(a\) radius \(c\)

$$\lvert z-a\rvert=\lvert z-b\rvert$$

where \(a\) and \(b\) are complex, describes the perpendicular bisector of the line through the points \(a\) and \(b\)

$$Arg(z)=\theta,-\pi\lt\theta\leq\pi$$

is a ray (half-line) at an angle of \(\theta\) with the positive direction of the \(Re(z)\) axis (note that \((0,0)\) is not included since \(Arg(z)\) is not defined); \(Arg(z-a)=\theta\), where \(a=a_1+a_2i\) and \(-\pi\lt\theta\leq\pi\), is a translation of the ray by \(a_1\) units in the horizontal direction and \(a_2\) units in the vertical direction

chapter 3 | differential calculus

inverse trigonometric functions

$$\frac{d}{dx}(\sin^{-1}(x))=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}(\cos^{-1}(x))=\frac{-1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}(\tan^{-1}(x))=\frac{1}{1+x^2}$$

second derivatives

if \(y=f(x)\), the second derivative is $$f''(x)\text{ or }\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$$

special points on the graph of \(y=f(x)\) can be found using properties of the second derivative

$$\text{if }\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\gt 0$$

then the graph is concave up

$$\text{if }\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\lt 0$$

then the graph is concave down

$$\text{if }\frac{dy}{dx}=0\text{ and }\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\lt 0\text{ when }x=a$$

then there is a maximum stationary point at at \(x=a\)

$$\text{if }\frac{dy}{dx}=0\text{ and }\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\gt 0\text{ when }x=a$$

then there is a minimum stationary point at \(x=a\)

$$\text{if }\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=0\text{ and }\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\text{ changes sign}\text{ when }x=a$$

then there is a point of inflection at \(x=a\)

implicit differentiation

use implicit differentiation when you are asked to differentiate a relation. to find \(\frac{dy}{dx}\), differentiate with respect to \(x\). use the chain rule when differentiating terms containing \(y\) and solve for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\)

related rates

use the chain rule in related rates of change problems

chapter 4 | integral calculus

log integration

$$\int\frac{1}{x}\,dx=\log_e\lvert x\rvert+c=\log_ex+c,x>0$$

inverse trigonometric functions

$$\int\frac{1}{\sqrt{a^2-x^2}}\,dx=\sin^{-1}(\frac{x}{a})+c$$

$$\int\frac{-1}{\sqrt{a^2-x^2}}\,dx=\cos^{-1}(\frac{x}{a})+c$$

$$\int\frac{a}{a^2+x^2}\,dx=\tan^{-1}(\frac{x}{a})+c$$

method of substitution

$$\int f(u)\frac{du}{dx}\,dx=\int f(u)\,du$$

where \(u\) is a function of \(x\)

$$\int_a^bf(u)\frac{du}{dx}\,dx=\int_{g(a)}^{g(b)}f(u)\,du$$

where \(u=g(x)\)

linear substitution

$$\int f(x)g(ax+b)\,dx=\frac{1}{a}\int f(\frac{u-b}{a})g(u)\,du$$

where \(u=ax+b\) and \(\frac{du}{dx}=\frac{1}{a}\)

trigonometric identities

$$\sin(2x)=2\sin x\cos x$$

$$\cos^2(ax)=\frac{1}{2}(1+cos(2ax))$$

$$\sin^2(ax)=\frac{1}{2}(1-cos(2ax))$$

$$1+\tan^x=\sec^2x$$

partial fractions

$$\frac{ax+b}{(x-m)(x-n)}=\frac{A}{x-m}+\frac{B}{x-n}$$

$$\frac{ax+b}{(x-m)^2}=\frac{A}{(x-m)^2}+\frac{B}{x-m}$$

area

if \(f\) and \(g\) are continuous for \(x\in[a,b]\) and \(f(x)\geq g(x)\), then the area of the region bounded by the curves \(y=f(x)\), \(y=g(x)\) and the lines \(x=a\) and \(x=b\) is given by

$$\int_a^b(f(x)-g(x))\,dx$$

volume

if a region bounded by the curve \(y=f(x)\) and the lines \(x=a\) and \(x=b\) is rotated \(360^\circ\) about the x-axis, then the volume \(V\) is given by

$$V=\int_a^b\pi y^2\,dx=\int_a^b\pi(f(x))^2\,dx$$

if a region bounded by the curve \(x=g(y)\) and the lines \(y=c\) and \(y=d\) is rotated \(360^\circ\) about the y-axis, then the volume \(V\) is given by

$$V=\int_c^d\pi x^2\,dy=\int_c^d\pi(g(y))^2\,dy$$

if a region bounded by the curves \(y=f(x)\) and \(y=g(x)\) and the lines \(x=a\) and \(x=b\), where \(f(x)\geq g(x)\) on \([a,b]\), is rotated \(360^\circ\) about the x-axis, then the volume V is given by

$$V=\int_a^b\pi((f(x))^2-(g(x))^2)\,dx$$

graphs of anti-derivative functions

at points where the graph of a function crosses the x-axis, the graphs of its anti-derivative functions will have a turning point

at points where the graphs of a function touches (but does not cross) the x-axis, the graphs of its anti-derivative will have a horizontal point of inflection

arc length

if \(y=f(x)\) and \(L\) is the length of the curve (arc length) between the point where \(x=a\) and the point where \(x=b\), then \(L\) is given by

$$L=\int_a^b\sqrt{1+(\frac{dy}{dx})^2}\,dx$$

$$L=\int_a^b\sqrt{1+(f'(x))^2}\,dx$$
